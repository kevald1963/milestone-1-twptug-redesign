A Brief History of Deregulation

The Transport Act 1985 heralded bus deregulation in the UK, which transferred operation of bus services from public bodies to private companies. Road road service licensing was abolished and local bus services were allowed to compete for the first time since the 1930s.

This led to cut-throat competition and bus wars in some areas. Tactics included cutting fares and operating extra services. Smaller operators went bust, or were taken over by the larger transport companies.

As at 2010 the big five operators, Arriva, First, Go-Ahead, National Express and Stagecoach, controlled 70% of the market. Bus deregulation has effectively turned local public monopolies, such as the PTEs, into local private monopolies.

For more details see Bus deregulation in Great Britain

The Case for Reregulation


Bus ridership is in decline

When the Conservative government de-regulated bus services in the mid 80’s one of their stated ambitions was to halt the decline in ridership over the previous thirty years. They claimed that public ownership and council-led bus services were to blame and that private corporations would be more in touch with the needs of bus users. That view is still peddled today, despite all the evidence to the contrary, that bus use has continued to decline. To understand the decline in ridership, we have to look further back to the Fifties and the post war generation. They really fell in love with the motor car but finance and credit was difficult to get in those days so the vast majority could not afford to buy a new car, or to even run a second-hand car. It was largely a privilege of the middle and upper classes.

With increasing prosperity, car-ownership continued to slowly rise through the decades, but took on a huge spurt with the growth in easy access to finance and credit in the 80s. Those who could, bought a car, while the rest, the elderly, the vulnerable, the marginalised were left waiting at the bus stop for the privately run service. The question which the Conservative government did not bother to ask was ‘How could the private bus operators make a profit out of the poorer sections of society?'. The bus operators clearly could not. That’s why buses, despite government claims since, cannot be run on a commercial basis; they need public subsidy. Collectively we all pay nearly a third of the cost for the bus services that operate throughout Tyne & Wear.


Bus operators will not integrate their fares with each other

While there is good physical integration of public transport services between buses, Metro and Ferry here in Tyne & Wear, fares integration leaves a lot to be desired. Buses will issue Nexus transfares and day tickets that can also be used on Metro or Ferry, but generally not for the services of other bus operators. This is not only makes longer bus-to-bus journeys more expensive for users but further undermines the competitiveness of buses with private cars, the economics of which, is already skewed in favour of cars.

A publicly-accountable, local government body could integrate all fares and across Tyne & Wear with a system similar to the Oyster travel cards used in London. We are not necessarily calling for the bus operating companies to be taken into local public ownership, but for them to run their services on behalf of the local transport authority through a franchising or quality contract scheme. The bus operators would still be allowed to make a profit, as well as be giving the opportunity to grow their business through increased ridership.


Tackling congestion and pollution

The combination of deteriorating air quality and rising traffic congestion levels in the UK’s major cities is not sustainable and will, inevitably, drive change in how people commute and move around. Buses can play a key and growing role in that change as the leading solution along key arterial corridors into and out of major cities where the ability for a large number of people travelling in the same direction to share one vehicle will be the most efficient, least polluting, ride sharing option.
